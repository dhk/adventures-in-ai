How Does Trump Have a Hold on the Christian Right?
John Taylor · The Washington Post (The 7)  ·  May 9, 2026
Key Summary Points
0171% of White evangelicals approved of Trump in a new Washington Post poll — identical to the February 2026 figure — despite an overwhelmingly negative response to religion-related messages from Trump and his administration.
02Trump has 49% approval among those who attend religious services weekly or more, compared with 37% for monthly or annual attendees and 32% for those who seldom or never attend — religious practice frequency tracks Trump support.
03Trump's overall disapproval hit 62% in the poll — described by polling director Scott Clement as "a record high for his 5 1/2 years as president."
04Some Democrats see an opening with religious voters and are encouraging candidates to talk more about their own faith — evangelical pastor Doug Pagitt's pitch: "not to become a Democrat but to vote for one in the next election."
05For more than a decade, Trump has presented himself as a champion of the deeply religious, rewarded with broad support from White Christians — some of whom compare him with biblical heroes.

Key Takeaways
White evangelical support is sticky even as overall disapproval hits record highs — the sub-group remains largely decoupled from broader national sentiment.
The frequency-of-practice gradient (49% weekly → 32% seldom) suggests Trump's appeal is strongest among the most religiously embedded voters, not casual believers.
62% overall disapproval is historically significant — it creates genuine electoral risk across general election contests even if primary bases remain loyal.
Democrats' faith outreach strategy ("lend your vote this one time") is explicitly modest — not a conversion play but a permission-giving one, trying to lower the cost of a single defection.
The persistence of White evangelical support despite negative views of religion-related messaging suggests identity/tribal loyalty now functions semi-independently of specific policy or behavior.
