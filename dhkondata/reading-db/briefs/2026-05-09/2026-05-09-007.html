Microbiome Startup Raises $48M for Immunotherapy and Malnutrition Treatments
Ryan Cross · Endpoints News  ·  May 9, 2026
Key Summary Points
01Kanvas Biosciences identified ~50 bacterial strains from a cancer survivor's stool that collectively created conditions for immunotherapy to cure her advanced colorectal cancer — and figured out how to grow that microbial community in the lab.
02Kanvas raised $48M in a Series A to run a clinical trial later this year testing these bacterial pills in immunotherapy patients — plus fund two additional microbe-based therapies.
03The second program targets colon inflammation caused by immunotherapy; the third is a Gates Foundation-backed therapy aimed at healing guts of patients with malnutrition/malabsorption.
04The scientific insight: cancer immunotherapy efficacy varies dramatically between patients — gut microbiome conditions appear to be a major, previously underweighted variable in immune response.
05The "super poop" source — a single exceptional cancer survivor — provided the bacterial blueprint; the challenge is replicating those conditions reliably in packaged form at clinical scale.
newsletter / healthcare  ·  Endpoints Weekly
Key Takeaways
The microbiome-immunotherapy connection is moving from hypothesis to clinical asset — Kanvas is among the first to translate a specific patient's gut biology into a packaged therapeutic.
Studying extreme responders ("exceptional survivors") as a drug discovery strategy is increasingly productive — this is a model for precision oncology beyond genomics.
The Gates Foundation backing for the malnutrition program signals microbiome therapies may have global-health applications beyond affluent-world oncology.
Clinical manufacturing and consistency of a ~50-strain bacterial mixture will be the key technical hurdle — not the science itself, but the reproducible production of a complex living product.
If the immunotherapy-enhancement trial succeeds, it could reframe patient selection for checkpoint inhibitors — gut microbiome profiling could become standard pre-treatment evaluation.
