Redistricting, Primaries, and Trump's Grip on the GOP
Rachael Larimore · The Dispatch  ·  May 9, 2026
Key Summary Points
01Six of eight Trump-endorsed challengers defeated Indiana Republican state senators who had refused to support redistricting in a special session — one incumbent survived and one race remained too close to call.
02Thomas Massie (KY-4) faces a May 19 primary challenge from Trump-endorsed dairy farmer Ed Gallrein; Massie's biggest affront was pushing the Epstein Files Transparency Act, which passed 427-1 in the House and unanimously in the Senate before Trump signed it.
03Iowa's June 2 Democratic Senate primary pits progressive Zach Wahls against establishment-backed Josh Turek, who has more than $5.5 million in backing from a Schumer-affiliated super PAC, VoteVets.
04The Supreme Court struck down Louisiana's congressional map in Louisiana v. Callais as an unconstitutional racial gerrymander, forcing redistricting in Louisiana, Alabama, and Tennessee ahead of the midterms.
05Louisiana Gov. Jeff Landry postponed the state's May 16 primary to give the legislature time to draw new maps following the SCOTUS ruling.

Key Takeaways
Trump's endorsement still drives primary outcomes even with record-low approval — MAGA retribution primaries remain effective at party discipline, especially in state-level races.
Massie's case is a test of whether genuine libertarian-leaning dissent can survive in Trump's GOP — if he loses, the Epstein-files move proves to be politically fatal.
Iowa's Democratic primary is a leading indicator: does the party run progressive candidates against sentiment or establishment-backed candidates against electability concerns?
Louisiana v. Callais adds map chaos to multiple Southern states ahead of midterms — redistricting fights under court deadlines create unpredictable electoral geographies.
The article's frame: both parties face internal divisions, but the GOP's is enforced through primary retribution, while Democrats' plays out in competitive primaries on ideology.
