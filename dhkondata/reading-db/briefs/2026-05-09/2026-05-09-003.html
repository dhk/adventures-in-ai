The End of Elsewhere: Why Travel Has Lost Its Allure
Grant David Crawford via The Weekender  ·  May 9, 2026
Key Summary Points
01Crawford teaches travel ethics and noticed ~2 years ago that students stopped arguing about it — not because they're less ethical or intelligent, but because "the question itself has lost its hold on them."
02International student enrollment fell 17% in fall 2025 — the largest non-pandemic decline on record — removing a key cosmopolitan experience for American undergraduates without leaving the country.
03Crawford argues the disengagement is not economic (students who can't afford travel still typically want to travel) but reflects "a loss of belief" — the concept of a meaningful "elsewhere" no longer compels.
04Nabeel Qureshi argues great art subverts expectations at every level — LLMs predict high-probability words, producing dullness; great art (Macbeth's "multitudinous seas incarnadine") violates expectations satisfyingly.
05"Authenticity is a story the tourist tells about herself, not a property of the place she visits" — Crawford's core thesis, drawing on David Sze's "Myth of Authentic Travel."

Key Takeaways
Travel's loss of appeal in younger Westerners may be a leading indicator of a broader retreat from cosmopolitanism — not just economics, but a deeper epistemic shift.
AI's structural inability to surprise is a genuine artistic limitation — not a training-data problem — because it optimizes for high-probability outputs by design.
Both the travel and art pieces converge on self-illusion: "authenticity" in travel and "surprise" in art are both about the perceiver's construction, not an external property.
The Met Gala cringe piece mirrors the travel thesis: when everyone performs subversion, it becomes predictable — the "try-hard" gala moment mirrors the "authentic traveler" myth.
Crawford's classroom is a real-world signal, not just an anecdote — generational shifts in engagement with cosmopolitan ideals may have measurable political consequences.
