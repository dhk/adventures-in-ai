Timothy Garton Ash on Europe's Political Fragmentation
Yascha Mounk (Persuasion)  ·  May 9, 2026
Key Summary Points
01Reform UK was the biggest winner in England's local/national elections, sweeping votes from both Labour and Conservatives — Britain now has a seven-party system, like continental Europe.
02Labour's 2024 "landslide" was partly an artifact of right-vote splitting; Labour's actual vote share was slightly down. Garton Ash says Keir Starmer "has made endless u-turns" and it is "totally unclear where they are going" with their parliamentary majority.
03Viktor Orbán was defeated at the ballot box in Hungary by a "massive outpouring of popular will" despite the non-level playing field — TISZA won a constitutional majority partly because EU membership constrained Orbán's options for nullifying the result.
04The likely next French president is either Marine Le Pen or Jordan Bardella (RN); Macron's camp is overconfident, echoing Democrats pre-2016 Trump underestimation.
05Europe's structural problem: "the policies we need are European but the politics are still national" — defense, capital markets, digital space all need EU-scale action that national politics blocks.

Key Takeaways
Britain's fragmentation into a seven-party system mirrors European continental patterns — Reform UK is structurally similar to Fratelli d'Italia and Rassemblement National.
Labour's crisis is programmatic, not tactical: having a massive majority but no coherent agenda is its core vulnerability.
The Orbán defeat is a proof-of-concept: elections remain dangerous moments even for competitive authoritarian systems, especially within the EU's external constraints.
Garton Ash's "Besitzstandswahrung" thesis: Europeans are too comfortable to reform, but comfortable decline may turn suddenly acute — like going bankrupt gradually then all at once.
The Bardella-as-president scenario is not being taken seriously enough in Macronist circles — a potentially costly miscalculation given 2016-Trump precedent.
