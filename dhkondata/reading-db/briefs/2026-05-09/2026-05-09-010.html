Ohio in a Handbasket: The Rise and Fall of Longaberger
Jonny Gartner · The Dispatch Culture  ·  May 9, 2026
Key Summary Points
01Longaberger Basket Company was founded in 1972 in Dresden, Ohio with just five weavers; by 2000 it had more than $1 billion in annual sales, 8,000 direct employees, and 70,000 sales associates nationwide.
02The company's basket-shaped headquarters — a 180,000 sq ft replica of a Longaberger basket — now sits vacant outside Newark, Ohio, nearly 10 years after the company's 2016 closure and 2018 bankruptcy filing.
03Longaberger grew through multilevel marketing alongside Tupperware and similar companies — the family's nearly 100-year-old basket-making craft was the foundation of a direct-sales empire.
04The vacant building is one of many abandoned manufacturing plants scattered across Ohio — the author describes the state as "littered" with such sites, testament to a rapidly changing economic landscape.
05The building persists due to nostalgia and hopes for future development — it remains "an artifact of a bygone era," a symbol of agricultural and manufacturing Ohio becoming something new.

Key Takeaways
Longaberger's arc — from five weavers to $1B in sales to abandoned building — is a compressed version of American Midwestern manufacturing's broader story.
The MLM model's alignment with handcraft nostalgia was a powerful combination that collapsed when the cultural moment passed — "authenticity" was always the brand's core asset, and authenticity ages.
The basket-shaped HQ building as monument and ruin is a uniquely American artifact: companies build monuments to success, then leave them when they fail, letting nostalgia do the preservation work.
Ohio's deindustrialization is still ongoing — the basket company is one data point in a state that has been "becoming something new" for several decades without fully articulating what that new thing is.
The personal inheritance frame (the grandmother's mail basket on the author's desk) is what elevates this from economic history to cultural memory — these objects carry meaning long after the company is gone.
