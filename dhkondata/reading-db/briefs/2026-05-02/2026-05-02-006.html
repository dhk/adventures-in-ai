Lant Pritchett on Why Foreign Aid Misses the Point
Yascha Mounk · Persuasion  ·  May 2, 2026
Key Summary Points
01Pritchett defines real development as a fourfold national transformation — high-productivity economy, state capability, citizen-responsive polity, and rule of law — not the project-by-project poverty mitigation that most aid agencies actually fund.
02The "bird on the elephant" model: aid money (the elephant) is roughly neutral in its impact, but the dominant ideas and discourse (the bird) shape whether development happens — getting development economics' ideas wrong is more damaging than any specific aid misallocation.
03RCTs are "ontologically wrong" for development: they study individuals when the problem is systemic. "People in Niger are poor because they are in Niger" — not because of personal deficits that individuated interventions can fix. Moving a Nigerien to France converges their income almost instantly.
04Vietnam went from $1.80/day to $7.50/day per capita through embedding Vietnamese labor in global value chains — not anti-poverty programs. China's transformation similarly happened when Deng changed development strategy based on ideas, not when aid levels changed.
05Pritchett's "full Trinity growth diagnostics" — identifying binding constraints that are technically correct, organizationally achievable, and politically supportable — is the neglected alternative. Different countries produce different answers; if every diagnostic produces the same answer, it's ideology.

Key Takeaways
The RCT critique is fundamental, not methodological: RCTs can tell you whether a village-level intervention works within a broken system — they cannot fix the system, which is the actual development problem.
The "bird on the elephant" consequence: brilliant economists doing deworming studies may be net-negative if they draw talent away from the macro-level ideas that actually change national development trajectories.
An underappreciated empirical fact: the last 60 years are the greatest period of human material progress in history — child mortality from 200/1000 to ~30, education from 2 to 8 years average. This happened, which proves it is possible.
"Inclusion into productivity" reframes development from redistribution to system design — the question is how to embed more people in more sophisticated value-creating cooperative structures, not how to transfer money to individuals.
The Dani Rodrik trajectory (WTO critique of 2000 → shared prosperity concerns of 2026) illustrates Mounk's observation: we had the opposite development problem from what the progressive critique predicted — and the WTO was good for humanity, if not Michigan.
