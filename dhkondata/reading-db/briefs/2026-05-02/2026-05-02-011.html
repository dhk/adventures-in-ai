Intellia's In Vivo CRISPR Therapy First to Succeed in Phase 3
Ryan Cross & Lei Lei Wu · Endpoints News  ·  May 2, 2026
Key Summary Points
01Intellia Therapeutics' in vivo CRISPR therapy became the first of its kind to succeed in a Phase 3 trial — treating hereditary angioedema (HAE), a genetic disease causing unpredictable and potentially fatal swelling attacks.
02The one-time infusion, which edits genes directly inside the body, reduced swelling attacks by 87% compared to placebo — a clinically substantial efficacy signal for a disease with severe quality-of-life and safety consequences.
03The trial puts the therapy on track for potential FDA approval in the first half of 2027 — marking a near-14-year milestone from CRISPR's discovery to likely clinical deployment as an approved drug.
04Despite the Phase 3 success, skepticism about CRISPR's scale of impact in medicine is growing — HAE is a rare monogenic disease, and questions remain about whether the technology can address the broader disease targets that originally drove its promise.
05In vivo CRISPR (editing genes inside the body) is technically distinct from ex vivo (editing extracted cells then reinfusing them) — this Phase 3 win advances the more challenging, higher-scalability branch of CRISPR therapeutics.

Key Takeaways
Phase 3 success for in vivo CRISPR is a genuine scientific milestone — it validates a delivery and editing mechanism that was theoretical 5 years ago, not an incremental improvement on existing gene therapy approaches.
The 87% reduction in HAE attacks is clinically transformative: HAE has no reliable trigger, can be life-threatening, and dramatically limits patient freedom — a near-complete response in a single infusion is a meaningful clinical outcome.
The "skepticism growing" signal is worth tracking: CRISPR's original promise (cures for thousands of diseases) may be narrowing in practice to specific rare monogenic conditions — a still-important but more limited scope than initially envisioned.
In vivo vs. ex vivo is the key strategic distinction — in vivo is harder and riskier but more scalable and broadly applicable; this Phase 3 result advances the more ambitious and potentially more impactful branch of CRISPR therapeutics.
FDA approval in H1 2027 puts Intellia in a competitive race with other gene therapy modalities (siRNA, base editing, prime editing) — clinical success in CRISPR accelerates the entire competitive field, not just Intellia's position.
