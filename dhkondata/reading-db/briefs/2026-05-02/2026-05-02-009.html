FDA Claims Trial Manipulation Tainted Approval of Amgen's Tavneos
Nicole DeFeudis & Lei Lei Wu · Endpoints News  ·  May 2, 2026
Key Summary Points
01The FDA formally accused ChemoCentryx study personnel of manipulating results of the pivotal trial used to approve Tavneos (avacopan) in 2021 for ANCA-associated vasculitis — escalating its effort to force the drug off the market.
02Amgen acquired ChemoCentryx for $4 billion in 2022, inheriting both the drug and now the regulatory dispute; when FDA asked Amgen to voluntarily withdraw at the start of 2026, Amgen refused.
03CDER's formal withdrawal proposal states the marketing application contained "untrue statements of material fact" and that Tavneos "has not been shown to be effective for its approved use" — the strongest possible regulatory language short of criminal referral.
04The FDA added new safety concerns: it is "increasingly concerned" about post-market cases of drug-induced liver injury, including vanishing bile duct syndrome — a serious, potentially irreversible complication that Amgen filed a label supplement to add warnings for.
05Amgen's Q1 earnings report reiterated that it continues to believe Tavneos "demonstrates effectiveness and a favorable benefit-risk profile" while engaging with FDA — signaling a formal dispute process, not a voluntary exit.

Key Takeaways
An FDA data-integrity accusation against a post-acquisition drug is rare and structurally awkward: Amgen must defend a drug it didn't develop using trial data it didn't generate — inherited liability at the highest possible regulatory severity.
The $4B acquisition price now looks like a significant liability: Amgen bought not just the drug but its evidentiary problems, at a price that assumed a clean regulatory track record.
Escalating safety concerns (vanishing bile duct syndrome) add a patient safety dimension beyond the efficacy dispute — making Amgen's "favorable benefit-risk" assertion increasingly difficult to sustain publicly, even if legally defensible.
If FDA succeeds in forcing withdrawal, it would be a major precedent for post-approval data integrity scrutiny — potentially chilling the market for acquiring clinical-stage assets with complex regulatory histories.
This case illustrates a specific acquisition risk: inheriting a drug near or past approval means inheriting its regulatory track record, including deficiencies the acquirer had no hand in creating and no ability to remediate retroactively.
