California's "Billionaire Tax" Is the Wrong Approach
Noah Smith · Noahpinion  ·  May 2, 2026
Key Summary Points
01California's proposed one-time 5% levy on net worth over $1 billion — earmarked for health care, education, and food assistance over five years — is "silly" per Smith despite his general support for taxing the rich more and progressive taxation broadly.
02A one-time levy creates fiscal unsustainability: programs funded this way run out in 5 years, forcing a second confiscation, a rate hike, or program cuts — a structural "time bomb" that sets up a future political crisis rather than solving one.
03State-level wealth taxes fail because billionaires relocate: Moretti and Wilson (2023) found that when a state implements an estate tax, ~35% of Forbes 400 billionaires move to another state — the mobility constraint is hard empirical evidence, not libertarian theory.
04The proposal exemplifies "slopulism" — the Democratic tendency to imagine government can be funded entirely on the super-rich while the merely-rich get tax cuts, a trend Smith sees as both intellectually dishonest and politically corrosive.
05Smith's preferred alternative: raise ongoing marginal tax rates on high earners (as Massachusetts does for incomes over $1M) rather than one-off confiscations — predictable, sustainable, and harder to evade through relocation.

Key Takeaways
The billionaire-tax debate conflates two separable questions: "Should the super-rich pay more?" (Smith: yes) and "Is this the right mechanism?" (Smith: no) — treating them as one question muddles the policy debate.
One-time levies are structurally equivalent to Congressional "temporary" tax cuts — they kick the can and set up future political crises rather than building durable revenue streams.
The 35% billionaire-relocation finding is the decisive empirical constraint: it means state-level wealth taxes have a self-defeating logic — the levy's existence accelerates the exit of the base it taxes.
"Slopulism" is a useful diagnostic concept: it captures a specific failure mode where progressive ambition toward redistribution outpaces seriousness about the mechanics of sustainable revenue design.
The durable alternative (permanent rate increases on high earners) is less dramatic and therefore less politically rewarding — which is exactly why "slopulist" proposals keep crowding it out of the conversation.
