Why ChatGPT Is Obsessed With Goblins: The Weirdest Possible Explanation
Katherine Dee · The Weekender (Pirate Wires)  ·  May 2, 2026
Key Summary Points
01OpenAI's Codex system prompt had to explicitly prohibit ChatGPT from mentioning "goblins, gremlins, trolls, ogres, pigeons, and raccoons unless absolutely and unambiguously relevant" — because the model was genuinely fixated on them. Goblin usage was up 175% since GPT-5.1 launched.
02Root cause: the "Nerdy" persona preset (just 2.5% of ChatGPT traffic) had a reinforcement learning reward signal that was inadvertently scoring goblin-containing outputs higher — the model was being rewarded every time it called something a goblin.
03Rewarded "Nerdy" outputs were recycled into supervised fine-tuning data for the base model, causing the goblin tic to escape the persona entirely — dragging an "enchanted forest" of adjacent creatures (raccoons, ogres, pigeons) with it.
04OpenAI killed the offending reward gradient in March and purged affected vocabulary from training data, but GPT-5.5 (Codex) was already in training — requiring the explicit system prompt prohibition as a workaround.
05The post-mortem doesn't explain the most interesting question: why goblins specifically? When rewarded for "playful/Nerdy" outputs, the model didn't drift toward random mythological vocabulary — it gravitated toward "the small mischievous category of thing that lives in your walls and steals small objects."

Key Takeaways
This is a clean empirical case study in how stylistic tics propagate through training pipelines — a persona-level reward signal contaminated the base model through SFT data recycling, a risk with implications beyond goblins.
The "enchanted forest" problem illustrates how reward signals generalize unpredictably: a reward for one vocabulary cluster hitched adjacent thematic vocabulary along for the ride.
The gap between "kill the bad gradient" and the deployed model is a systemic risk: training pipelines have long lag times, meaning errors discovered late are already baked into models in production.
The unexplained specificity — goblins, not unicorns — is the most scientifically interesting residual question. It suggests RLHF optimization for "Nerdy/playful" activates a latent thematic cluster in the training corpus, not random mythological vocabulary.
The public sharing of the "goblin unlock" bash command is a characteristically internet-era moment: a training artifact becomes a feature, then a meme, then documentation — within a single news cycle.
