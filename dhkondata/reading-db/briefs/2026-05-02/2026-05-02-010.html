Should Pharma Be Swinging Bigger With AI?
Andrew Dunn · Endpoints News  ·  May 2, 2026
Key Summary Points
01At ICLR (International Conference on Learning Representations) in Rio de Janeiro — one of the world's largest AI research gatherings — Roche veteran Kyunghyun Cho argued that biopharma's AI ambitions are fundamentally too narrow.
02Current pharma AI focus: shaving months off drug development timelines, streamlining data analysis, flagging safety signals — incremental efficiency gains, not structural transformation of drug development.
03Cho's vision: AI that upends the basic paradigm itself — shifting from "drugs that somewhat help a percentage of patients" to treatments that work well for a defined sub-population at scale, i.e., precision medicine delivered at broad population scale.
04The barrier Cho identifies isn't technical capability: "We've got to lower the barrier between the pharmaceutical and the care providers, because the incentives are not aligned despite the fact that the technology is actually getting better."
05The ICLR venue matters: Cho is making this argument at an ML conference, not a pharma conference — signaling that AI research community is running ahead of the industry in thinking about AI's structural potential in biomedicine.

Key Takeaways
The "thinking too small" critique is now coming from inside the research community with pharma credentials, not just from AI boosters — Cho's Roche background gives the argument operational credibility it wouldn't otherwise have.
The incentive-alignment problem is the real constraint: pharma is paid for drugs approved and broadly prescribed, not for drugs maximally effective in sub-populations — AI can't fix that without business model changes, not just technical ones.
"Shaving months" vs. "upending the paradigm" is a classic innovation vs. optimization distinction — incremental AI captures most pharma investment while genuinely transformational applications remain underfunded and under-imagined.
If AI enables precision medicine at scale, the commercial question becomes: who captures the value — the algorithm developer, the pharma company, or the payer/insurer? The business model question is as hard as the technical one.
The gap between AI research capability and pharma deployment is the current defining tension in biomedical AI — ICLR is where the ideas are being developed; pharma boardrooms are where the deployment decisions are being deferred.
